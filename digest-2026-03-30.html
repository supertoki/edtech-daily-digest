Key Themes & Trends

1. The AI Use-Case Gap Is Real and Persistent

EdSurge's top story on March 27 is the sharpest new signal today: teachers still cannot identify a compelling AI use case for their classrooms. This is remarkable given two-plus years of intense AI hype and product launches. It suggests the edtech industry and professional development ecosystem have failed to translate AI capability into classroom-legible value. The simultaneous faculty resistance to institutional OpenAI contracts (Inside Higher Ed) shows the same failure at the institutional level — adoption is being pushed from above, not pulled from below.

2. Teacher Compensation Crisis: Now a Multi-Source Theme

Three sources converge on teacher economics today: EdTech Digest's pay scale explainer reveals teachers losing six-figure lifetime income through system ignorance; Inside Higher Ed's CUPA-HR data shows admin compensation outpacing inflation while faculty salaries stagnate; and Brookings (prior day) documented alarming drops in teacher working conditions. This convergence across three independent sources is a signal, not coincidence — teacher economic precarity is becoming a structural policy crisis with direct edtech implications (burned-out, underpaid teachers are not enthusiastic technology adopters).

3. Graduate ROI: More Complicated Than the Headline

Brookings' "Graduate degrees pay — but it's complicated" adds important nuance to a narrative that drives enormous higher ed investment decisions. If returns are highly variable by field and institution, then the push to credential everyone into graduate education (a common edtech and workforce platform pitch) may be actively misallocating capital. This has direct implications for platform providers marketing grad school as the solution to economic mobility.

4. Federal Policy: Fracture Without Resolution

Inside Higher Ed's congressional hearing coverage shows a committee that cannot agree on what higher education's problems even are: Republicans see Chinese espionage, Democrats see student debt and ED layoffs. Neither conversation addresses AI governance, teacher workforce conditions, or campus financial fragility. The IES revival signal is the one positive note in an otherwise fragmented federal picture.

5. Privacy and Ethics Moving to the Foreground

Education Technology Insights today leads with privacy and responsible K-12 edtech ethics — a shift from pure feature coverage. Combined with THE Journal's shadow AI risk story and identity security trade-off analysis, privacy governance is emerging as an equal-weight concern alongside AI adoption, not an afterthought. Expect privacy frameworks to become procurement requirements, not optional audits.

6. International Perspective: Peru and the Global Learning Agenda

Brookings' Peru research is a quiet but important signal that the education outcomes conversation extends beyond U.S. federal policy battles. As global edtech platforms expand into emerging markets, understanding what "learning what matters" means in different national contexts becomes a product design and impact measurement imperative — not just a CSR talking point.
