Key Themes & Trends

1. Federal Higher Ed Policy Is Actively Contracting

Today's Inside Higher Ed cluster is the most consequential: MSI programs cut from grant eligibility, area studies centers losing Title VI funding, and law schools forced to become their own lenders under OBBBA loan caps. Brookings' OBBBA student loan analysis adds structural depth — this is not a series of isolated cuts but a coordinated reshaping of federal higher education investment. Institutions serving underrepresented students are bearing disproportionate impact.

2. AI Literacy Is Becoming a Workforce Policy Standard

The Department of Labor's five-area AI literacy framework (THE Journal, March 5) represents a milestone: AI competency is no longer just an industry preference but is entering formal workforce policy. Combined with the University of Richmond's new Center for Liberal Arts and AI — which deliberately embeds AI into non-STEM disciplines — the field is moving toward broad-based AI literacy as an institutional expectation, not a specialty track.

3. Screen Time: From Editorial to Legislative Battleground

EdSurge's continued screen-time coverage reflects a real policy shift underway. The debate has moved from educator opinion to active legislative chambers in multiple states. The tension for districts is acute: federal and state technology initiatives continue pushing device adoption, while a separate wave of state legislators is actively restricting it. Districts are caught between contradictory mandates with no clear federal guidance.

4. The Equity Fault Lines Are Widening Simultaneously

Three distinct equity pressures converge today: MSI funding exclusions (Inside Higher Ed), connectivity-as-prerequisite-for-AI-access (EdTech Digest), and the screen-time legislative push which disproportionately affects under-resourced districts that rely more heavily on school devices. The simultaneity matters — equity is not one problem but several compounding at once.

5. AI Governance: Noise Management Before Implementation

Both EdTech Digest ("Cutting Through the AI Noise") and THE Journal's shadow AI coverage frame the same problem differently: institutions are drowning in AI vendor claims and unsanctioned tool adoption before governance frameworks exist. The SETDA toolkit addresses evaluation; the DOL framework addresses competency; but the gap between policy and practice in individual districts remains wide.

6. Data Infrastructure as a Durable District-Controlled Asset

Ed-Fi's impact stories — attendance dashboards in South Carolina, teacher support in Texas, literacy tracking, seamless transfers in Michigan — continue to demonstrate that data standardization produces concrete, measurable student outcomes. As federal investment contracts in other areas, the case for data infrastructure as a locally-owned, durable asset grows stronger by contrast.
