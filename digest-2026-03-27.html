Meanwhile, the University of Richmond's new Center for Liberal Arts and AI demonstrates a maturing institutional philosophy: embedding AI literacy across all disciplines rather than siloing it in computer science.

Key Themes & Trends

1. AI Governance Goes National — and Gets Complicated

The White House national AI policy framework and the concurrent report on AI's identity security trade-offs arrive together as a paired signal: AI governance is no longer just a district-level problem. The DOL's five AI literacy competencies, SETDA's evaluation toolkit, and EdTech Digest's "cutting through the noise" advice for IT leaders all reflect a field trying to establish governance infrastructure retroactively — after wide deployment has already occurred.

2. Federal Higher Ed Investment Is Contracting at Multiple Points Simultaneously

MSI grant exclusions, area studies defunding, OBBBA loan caps forcing law schools to become their own lenders — these are not isolated events. Brookings' OBBBA analysis adds structural framing: the federal government's role as financier of higher education is being actively narrowed. Institutions serving underrepresented students face the steepest exposure, and the simultaneity makes institutional planning increasingly difficult.

3. Screen Time: A Debate Deepening, Not Resolving

EdSurge's multi-part series — from legislative news to personal essay to pedagogical reflection — reveals a debate that is deepening rather than finding resolution. With legislators pushing screen-free policies in some states while federal programs push device access in others, districts face structural contradiction. The personal essays suggest the conversation is also becoming a cultural reckoning about childhood, attention, and what school is fundamentally for.

4. Liberal Arts and AI: The Richmond Embedded Model

The University of Richmond's new center deliberately embeds AI ethics and literacy across humanities and social sciences rather than siloing it in STEM. This signals institutional maturation: AI is being understood as a lens and a societal condition, not a technology track. As more institutions debate where AI belongs in the curriculum, the liberal arts integration model offers a compelling alternative to add-on AI certificates or electives.

5. The Equity Stack: Connectivity, Funding, and Access Compounding

Three equity dimensions converge across today's sources: connectivity as prerequisite for AI-era learning (EdTech Digest), MSI funding exclusions reducing institutional capacity (Inside Higher Ed), and screen-time restrictions that disproportionately affect districts relying on school devices (EdSurge). These are separate problems but they compound — equity in 2026 is a multi-layered infrastructure problem, not a single program fix.

6. Teacher Conditions as an EdTech Implementation Variable

Brookings' alarming working conditions survey sits in an important relationship to the rest of the digest: technology solutions are being designed for deployment by educators who are increasingly stretched, underpaid, and disengaged. The digital ghosting epidemic and the screen-time debate both involve teacher judgment and relationship as the key variable — yet teacher wellbeing as implementation infrastructure is rarely the headline. No EdTech solution scales without the people deploying it.
